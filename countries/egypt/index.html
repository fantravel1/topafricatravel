Egypt

Land of the Pharaohs, the Nile, and timeless wonders. Discover 5,000 years of civilization, from the Great Pyramids to the coral gardens of the Red Sea.

Why Visit Egypt?

A timeless destination where ancient wonders meet vibrant modern culture, all along the life-giving waters of the Nile.

Egypt stands as one of the world's most extraordinary travel destinations, offering an unmatched combination of ancient history, natural beauty, and cultural richness. Home to the only remaining wonder of the ancient world -- the Great Pyramid of Giza -- Egypt has captivated travelers for millennia. The country's monuments, temples, and tombs chronicle a civilization that flourished for over three thousand years and continues to reveal new secrets to archaeologists today.

Beyond its legendary antiquities, Egypt offers diverse experiences for every type of traveler. The Nile River, the longest in Africa, flows through the heart of the country, sustaining fertile valleys amid vast desert landscapes. A cruise along the Nile between Luxor and Aswan remains one of the most iconic journeys in world travel. Along the Red Sea coast, world-class coral reefs attract divers and snorkelers from around the globe, while the Mediterranean city of Alexandria blends ancient heritage with coastal charm.

Egyptian culture is equally compelling. Cairo's bustling bazaars, aromatic street food, and warm hospitality create vivid sensory experiences. The country's Islamic and Coptic heritage adds layers of architectural splendor, from medieval mosques to ancient churches. Whether you come for the monuments, the diving, the desert, or the culture, Egypt delivers an experience that stays with you long after you leave.

Geography & Regions

From the lush Nile Delta to the vast Saharan wilderness, Egypt's diverse landscapes offer dramatically different travel experiences across four distinct regions.

Nile Valley & Delta

The Nile Valley is the cradle of Egyptian civilization and remains the country's beating heart. Stretching over 1,500 kilometers from Aswan in the south to the Mediterranean coast, this narrow fertile corridor is home to over 95% of Egypt's population. Cairo, the colossal capital of nearly 22 million people, dominates the apex of the Nile Delta where the river fans out into a lush green triangle before meeting the sea. Upriver, Luxor and Aswan preserve the grandest monuments of antiquity along the riverbanks. The delta region itself is a patchwork of farmland, palm groves, and small towns largely untouched by tourism, offering authentic glimpses of rural Egyptian life. The agricultural rhythm here has barely changed in thousands of years -- farmers still use irrigation channels descended from ancient designs, and feluccas still drift along the same waterway that carried pharaonic barges laden with granite obelisks.

Western Desert

Egypt's Western Desert covers roughly two-thirds of the country's total land area and is part of the vast Sahara that stretches across North Africa. Far from being a monotonous sea of sand, this region holds some of Egypt's most extraordinary natural wonders and remote cultural gems. The Siwa Oasis, tucked near the Libyan border, is a palm-fringed paradise of freshwater springs, salt lakes, and mud-brick villages where a Berber-speaking community maintains traditions distinct from the rest of Egypt. The White Desert near Farafra features surreal chalk rock formations sculpted by millennia of wind erosion into mushroom shapes, pillars, and arches that glow under moonlight. Bahariya Oasis serves as the gateway to the Black Desert and the Valley of the Golden Mummies, where thousands of Greco-Roman mummies were discovered in 1996. Overnight camping under the Saharan stars, with no light pollution for hundreds of kilometers, is one of Egypt's most transcendent experiences.

Red Sea Coast

Egypt's Red Sea coastline runs for over 1,200 kilometers along the eastern edge of the country, flanked by rugged desert mountains that plunge dramatically into crystal-clear tropical waters. This region is internationally renowned as one of the world's premier diving and snorkeling destinations, thanks to its vibrant coral reef ecosystems, year-round warm water temperatures, and exceptional underwater visibility often exceeding 30 meters. Hurghada, the largest resort city on the coast, offers everything from budget-friendly beach holidays to luxury all-inclusive resorts. Further south, Marsa Alam provides access to more pristine and less-crowded dive sites, including encounters with dugongs, oceanic whitetip sharks, and spinner dolphins. On the Sinai side, Sharm el-Sheikh sits at the southern tip of the peninsula and is the gateway to Ras Mohammed National Park, consistently ranked among the top ten dive sites on Earth. The Red Sea coast also offers desert safari excursions, kitesurfing, and world-class windsurfing conditions, particularly around El Gouna and Dahab.

Sinai Peninsula

The Sinai Peninsula is a triangular wedge of land bridging Africa and Asia, bounded by the Gulf of Suez to the west and the Gulf of Aqaba to the east. This rugged, mountainous region holds deep spiritual significance for the Abrahamic faiths as the biblical setting of Moses and the Exodus. Mount Sinai (Jebel Musa), rising 2,285 meters above sea level, draws pilgrims and hikers who make the pre-dawn ascent to witness a breathtaking sunrise from its summit. At the mountain's base, St. Catherine's Monastery -- founded in the 6th century and one of the oldest continuously operating Christian monasteries in the world -- houses priceless icons, manuscripts, and the legendary Burning Bush. On the coast, Dahab is a laid-back Bedouin village turned traveler haven, famous for the Blue Hole, one of the world's most celebrated freediving and scuba sites. The Sinai interior is home to Bedouin communities who offer guided treks through spectacular wadis, hidden oases, and ancient rock inscriptions dating back thousands of years.

Top Destinations in Egypt

From ancient temple complexes to stunning coastal resorts, these are the must-visit places in Egypt.

Cairo & the Pyramids of Giza

Egypt's sprawling capital is the gateway to the iconic Pyramids of Giza and the Great Sphinx. Explore the world-class Egyptian Museum, wander the medieval lanes of Islamic Cairo, and experience the vibrant chaos of Khan el-Khalili bazaar. The new Grand Egyptian Museum near the pyramids houses the largest collection of ancient Egyptian artifacts ever assembled.

Luxor

Often called the world's greatest open-air museum, Luxor sits on the site of ancient Thebes. The east bank holds the magnificent Karnak and Luxor Temples, while the west bank reveals the Valley of the Kings, the Temple of Hatshepsut, and the Colossi of Memnon. This is where Tutankhamun's tomb was discovered in 1922.

Aswan

Egypt's sunniest southern city offers a more relaxed pace along the beautiful upper Nile. Visit the Philae Temple dedicated to the goddess Isis, sail on a traditional felucca at sunset, explore colorful Nubian villages, and marvel at the engineering of the Aswan High Dam. Aswan is also the departure point for Abu Simbel excursions.

Red Sea & Hurghada

The Red Sea coast is a paradise for divers and beach lovers. Hurghada and Sharm el-Sheikh offer world-class diving and snorkeling among vibrant coral reefs teeming with marine life. The crystal-clear waters, year-round sunshine, and diverse underwater ecosystems make this one of the top diving destinations on Earth.

Alexandria

Founded by Alexander the Great in 331 BC, this Mediterranean port city blends Greek, Roman, and Egyptian heritage. Visit the modern Bibliotheca Alexandrina (echoing the legendary ancient library), the Catacombs of Kom el-Shoqafa, Qaitbay Citadel, and enjoy fresh seafood along the sweeping Corniche waterfront promenade.

Abu Simbel

The twin rock-cut temples of Abu Simbel, built by Ramesses II in the 13th century BC, are among Egypt's most awe-inspiring monuments. Carved directly into a mountainside near Lake Nasser, the temples were famously relocated in the 1960s to save them from rising floodwaters -- one of the greatest feats of archaeological engineering ever accomplished.

Food & Cuisine

Egyptian cuisine is a rich tapestry of flavors shaped by thousands of years of history, blending pharaonic, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and African influences into dishes that are hearty, aromatic, and deeply satisfying.

🍲

Koshari

Egypt's beloved national dish is a carb-lover's dream: a generous bowl of rice, macaroni, lentils, and chickpeas topped with a tangy tomato-vinegar sauce, crispy fried onions, and optional fiery chili garlic sauce (shatta). This vegetarian street food staple is served from dedicated koshari shops across the country, where massive pots simmer all day long. Originating in the 19th century as an affordable, filling meal for workers, koshari has become a unifying dish enjoyed by all social classes. Each component is cooked separately before being layered together, creating a symphony of textures and flavors that is quintessentially Egyptian.

🥣

Ful Medames

Considered one of the oldest dishes in the world, ful medames consists of slow-cooked fava beans seasoned with olive oil, lemon juice, cumin, and garlic, served with warm bread for scooping. This protein-rich dish has been a breakfast staple in Egypt for thousands of years -- traces of fava beans have been found in pharaonic tombs. Street vendors sell ful from large copper pots that simmer overnight, and every Egyptian family has its own preferred variation. Some add tahini, others top it with a fried egg or chopped tomatoes. A plate of ful with fresh aish baladi bread and a glass of sweet tea is the quintessential Egyptian morning meal.

🍛

Molokhia

This distinctive green soup made from finely chopped jute leaves (Corchorus olitorius) is one of Egypt's most traditional dishes, with recipes dating back to the pharaonic era. The leaves are simmered in a rich chicken or rabbit broth and finished with a fragrant "ta'leya" -- a sizzling mixture of garlic, coriander, and ghee that is stirred in just before serving. Molokhia has a thick, slightly viscous texture that some compare to okra, and its earthy, herbaceous flavor is deeply comforting. It is traditionally served over white rice with chicken or rabbit on the side, and accompanied by bread, lemon wedges, and raw onions. Every Egyptian grandmother claims to make the best molokhia, and family recipes are passed down through generations.

🍖

Shawarma & Kofta

Egyptian shawarma features thinly sliced marinated meat -- typically beef, lamb, or chicken -- slow-roasted on a vertical rotisserie spit, then shaved off and wrapped in warm flatbread with tahini, pickled vegetables, and garlic sauce. Kofta, meanwhile, consists of seasoned ground meat (usually a blend of beef and lamb) mixed with onions, parsley, and warm spices, shaped onto metal skewers and grilled over charcoal. Egyptian kofta has a distinctive spice profile featuring cumin, coriander, and a touch of cinnamon that sets it apart from other Middle Eastern versions. Both dishes are ubiquitous across Egypt, from humble street carts to upscale restaurants, and are often part of a mixed grill platter served with rice, salad, and freshly baked bread.

🍅

Mahshi (Stuffed Vegetables)

Mahshi is an elaborate labor of love that graces Egyptian tables on special occasions and family gatherings. Vegetables -- including vine leaves, zucchini, eggplant, bell peppers, cabbage, and tomatoes -- are carefully hollowed out and stuffed with a fragrant filling of rice mixed with herbs, spices, and sometimes minced meat. The stuffed vegetables are then slowly braised in a tomato-based sauce until tender and infused with flavor. Egyptian mahshi is distinguished by its use of dill, which gives the dish its characteristic aromatic quality. Preparing mahshi is a communal activity, with family members gathering to fill and roll dozens of vegetable parcels, making the cooking process as social and meaningful as the meal itself.

🧈

Feteer Meshaltet

Often called "Egyptian pizza" or "Egyptian pie," feteer meshaltet is a flaky, multi-layered pastry that demonstrates remarkable culinary craftsmanship. The dough is stretched paper-thin by hand -- almost translucent -- then folded with generous amounts of butter or ghee (samna baladi) to create dozens of crispy, airy layers. Feteer can be served sweet with honey, powdered sugar, or cream, or savory with cheese, minced meat, and vegetables. This ancient pastry likely dates back to the pharaonic era and remains a beloved comfort food throughout Egypt. Dedicated feteer shops, particularly in the rural Nile Delta region, are considered pilgrimage-worthy by Egyptian food lovers, and watching a skilled feteer maker stretch and twirl the dough is a performance in itself.

🍞

Egyptian Bread (Aish Baladi)

Bread is so fundamental to Egyptian life that the Arabic word for it -- "aish" -- literally means "life." Aish baladi, the country's staple bread, is a round, pocket-forming flatbread made from whole wheat flour and baked in extremely hot brick ovens. Egyptians consume more bread per capita than almost any other nation on earth, and subsidized bread from government bakeries remains a cornerstone of daily life. Every neighborhood has its local bakery where fresh rounds emerge from clay ovens throughout the day. Beyond aish baladi, Egyptian bread varieties include aish shamsi (sun-risen bread), aish merahrah (made with fenugreek), and the paper-thin aish fino used for sandwiches. Bread accompanies every Egyptian meal and serves as the primary utensil for scooping dips, stews, and grilled meats.

🍰

Desserts: Om Ali, Basbousa & Kunafa

Egyptian desserts are gloriously rich and sweet, often soaked in sugar syrup and perfumed with rosewater or orange blossom water. Om Ali, Egypt's most famous dessert, is a warm bread pudding made with puff pastry or phyllo dough soaked in sweetened milk, studded with raisins, coconut, and nuts, then baked until golden and bubbling. Legend attributes its creation to a 13th-century queen celebrating a rival's defeat. Basbousa is a dense, moist semolina cake soaked in fragrant syrup and topped with almonds, found in every bakery and sweet shop across the country. Kunafa features shredded phyllo dough (kataifi) layered with cream or cheese, baked until crisp, and drenched in sweet syrup. Other beloved treats include balah el-sham (fried choux pastry in syrup), halawa (sesame confection), and the date-filled cookies called kahk, traditionally baked for Eid celebrations.

Best Experiences in Egypt

Unforgettable activities and adventures that make Egypt one of the world's most rewarding destinations.

Stand Before the Pyramids

Witnessing the Great Pyramids of Giza in person is a once-in-a-lifetime experience. These 4,500-year-old monuments remain the most recognizable structures on Earth. Visit at sunrise to see them bathed in golden light, or attend the evening sound and light show for a dramatic perspective on their history.

Cruise the Nile

A multi-day Nile cruise between Luxor and Aswan is the quintessential Egyptian journey. Glide past ancient temples, fertile riverbanks, and desert landscapes while visiting iconic sites along the way. Whether on a traditional dahabiya sailboat or a modern cruise ship, the Nile reveals Egypt at its most timeless.

Dive the Red Sea

The Red Sea offers some of the world's finest diving, with crystal-clear visibility, colorful coral walls, and abundant marine life including dolphins, sea turtles, and reef sharks. Famous dive sites include Ras Mohammed National Park, the SS Thistlegorm wreck, and the Brothers Islands.

Explore Ancient Temples

Egypt's temple complexes are staggering in scale and beauty. Walk through the hypostyle hall of Karnak with its 134 massive columns, marvel at the perfectly preserved reliefs at Abydos, and discover the celestial ceiling of Dendera's Temple of Hathor. Each temple tells a different chapter of Egypt's rich spiritual history.

Wander the Bazaars

Cairo's Khan el-Khalili bazaar has been a center of trade since the 14th century. Navigate its labyrinthine alleys filled with spices, perfumes, handcrafted lanterns, jewelry, and textiles. Stop for traditional mint tea or strong Egyptian coffee in a centuries-old coffeehouse and soak in the atmosphere.

Venture into the Desert

Egypt's Western Desert holds otherworldly landscapes. The White Desert's chalk formations create a surreal moonscape, while the Siwa Oasis offers palm-fringed springs and the ruins of an ancient oracle temple visited by Alexander the Great. Overnight desert camping under star-filled skies is an unforgettable experience.

Felucca Sailing on the Nile

Gliding along the Nile aboard a traditional felucca -- a graceful lateen-sailed wooden boat unchanged for centuries -- is one of Egypt's most peaceful and romantic experiences. In Aswan, sunset felucca rides around Elephantine Island and the botanical gardens of Kitchener's Island offer magical views of the desert hills and Nubian villages along the riverbanks. Longer overnight felucca trips between Aswan and Kom Ombo let you sleep under the stars on deck as the boat drifts gently downriver, stopping at riverside temples and villages along the way. The silence of wind-powered travel, the changing light on the water, and the timelessness of the scenery make felucca sailing the most authentic way to experience the Nile.

Hot Air Balloon Over Luxor

Rising before dawn to float silently over the West Bank of Luxor in a hot air balloon is one of Egypt's most spectacular experiences. As the sun climbs above the Theban Hills, the Valley of the Kings, the Temple of Hatshepsut, the Ramesseum, and the Colossi of Memnon are revealed in breathtaking panorama below. The patchwork of green farmland along the Nile contrasts dramatically with the amber desert beyond, and the scale of the ancient mortuary temples becomes truly apparent from above. Flights typically last 45 minutes to an hour and operate year-round at dawn when winds are calmest. This bird's-eye perspective on ancient Thebes is unforgettable and offers photographic opportunities unlike anything at ground level.

Scuba Diving in the Red Sea

Beyond casual reef snorkeling, Egypt's Red Sea is a world-class scuba destination that draws certified divers from every continent. The SS Thistlegorm, a World War II cargo ship sunk by German bombers in 1941, is one of the most famous wreck dives on Earth -- its holds still contain motorcycles, trucks, rifles, and railway locomotives frozen in time. Ras Mohammed National Park at the tip of the Sinai Peninsula features dramatic underwater walls dropping into deep blue abysses, swarming with barracuda, jackfish, and the occasional hammerhead shark. Liveaboard dive boats offer multi-day expeditions to remote offshore reefs like the Brothers Islands, Daedalus Reef, and Elphinstone, where pelagic encounters with oceanic whitetip sharks, manta rays, and whale sharks are possible. Warm water temperatures averaging 22-28 degrees Celsius and visibility often exceeding 30 meters make Egypt's Red Sea a year-round diving paradise.

History & Heritage

Egypt's history spans over five millennia, making it one of the longest continuously documented civilizations in human history. Every era has left its mark on the landscape, creating a living museum unlike anywhere else on Earth.

Ancient Egypt emerged around 3100 BC when King Narmer unified Upper and Lower Egypt, establishing the first dynasty and beginning a civilization that would endure for over three thousand years. The Old Kingdom (c. 2686-2181 BC) saw the construction of the Great Pyramids of Giza and the Sphinx, engineering achievements that remain awe-inspiring nearly five millennia later. The Middle Kingdom brought literary and artistic flourishing, while the New Kingdom (c. 1550-1070 BC) produced Egypt's greatest pharaohs -- Hatshepsut, one of the few female pharaohs who ruled for over two decades; Akhenaten, the revolutionary monotheist who moved the capital to Amarna; Tutankhamun, whose intact tomb captivated the modern world; and Ramesses II, the great builder whose monuments dominate Luxor and Abu Simbel. The pharaohs developed hieroglyphic writing, advanced mathematics and astronomy, sophisticated medicine, and monumental architecture that influenced civilizations across the ancient world. Their burial practices and beliefs about the afterlife produced some of humanity's greatest artistic treasures, now housed in museums from Cairo to London, Paris, and New York.

The Greco-Roman period began with Alexander the Great's conquest in 332 BC and the founding of Alexandria, which became the intellectual capital of the ancient world. The Ptolemaic dynasty, descended from Alexander's general Ptolemy, ruled Egypt for nearly three centuries, blending Greek and Egyptian traditions in art, religion, and governance. The Great Library and the Lighthouse of Alexandria -- one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World -- symbolized this era's cultural ambition. Cleopatra VII, the last active ruler of the Ptolemaic Kingdom, is perhaps the most famous woman of antiquity, whose political alliances with Julius Caesar and Mark Antony shaped the fate of the Roman Republic. After Cleopatra's defeat at the Battle of Actium in 31 BC, Egypt became a province of Rome and served as the empire's granary. Roman temples at Dendera, Philae, and Kom Ombo show the continuation of Egyptian religious traditions under Roman patronage, and early Christianity took deep root in Egypt, giving rise to the Coptic Church and the monastic movement that would spread throughout Christendom.

The Arab conquest of 641 AD transformed Egypt profoundly, introducing Islam and the Arabic language and founding Fustat, the settlement that would evolve into Cairo. The Fatimid Caliphate established Cairo (Al-Qahira, "The Victorious") in 969 AD as their imperial capital, building the magnificent Al-Azhar Mosque and University, which remains one of the oldest continuously operating universities in the world. The Ayyubid dynasty under Saladin built the Cairo Citadel and repelled Crusader invasions, while the Mamluk Sultanate (1250-1517) transformed Cairo into one of the greatest cities on Earth, adorning it with hundreds of mosques, madrasas, caravanserais, and ornate fountains that still define the old city's skyline. Islamic Cairo, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, contains the densest concentration of medieval Islamic architecture anywhere in the world, with landmarks like the Sultan Hassan Mosque, the Ibn Tulun Mosque, and the Al-Muizz Street historic district offering an unparalleled window into the golden age of Islamic civilization.

The Ottoman conquest of 1517 incorporated Egypt into the vast Ottoman Empire, though the country retained considerable autonomy under local governors. The pivotal modern transformation began with Muhammad Ali Pasha, an Albanian-born Ottoman commander who seized power in 1805 and launched an ambitious modernization program modeled on European industrial and military innovations. His dynasty ruled Egypt for over a century, overseeing the construction of the Suez Canal (completed in 1869) -- one of the most consequential engineering projects in world history, linking the Mediterranean and Red Seas and revolutionizing global trade. The canal's construction, however, also led to crippling debt and British occupation in 1882. The British protectorate lasted until formal independence in 1922, though British influence persisted until the 1952 revolution. This era left a distinctive architectural legacy, particularly in downtown Cairo and Alexandria, where grand Belle Epoque boulevards and European-style buildings stand alongside medieval Islamic quarters and pharaonic monuments.

The 1952 Free Officers Revolution, led by Gamal Abdel Nasser, overthrew the monarchy and established Egypt as a republic, launching an era of Arab nationalism, land reform, and the monumental Aswan High Dam project that tamed the Nile's annual floods and electrified the country. Nasser's nationalization of the Suez Canal in 1956 became a defining moment in post-colonial history. Under Anwar Sadat, Egypt made peace with Israel in the historic 1978 Camp David Accords, fundamentally reshaping Middle Eastern geopolitics. The 2011 revolution, part of the broader Arab Spring, saw millions gather in Cairo's Tahrir Square to demand political change, a moment that captivated the world. Today's Egypt balances its ancient heritage with rapid modernization, including the construction of a new administrative capital east of Cairo and the long-awaited opening of the Grand Egyptian Museum at Giza, set to become the world's largest archaeological museum and a new chapter in Egypt's eternal story.

Wildlife & Nature

Beneath Egypt's archaeological fame lies a surprising wealth of natural wonders, from world-renowned coral reefs to unique desert ecosystems and vital wetland habitats.

Red Sea Coral Reefs

The Red Sea's coral reefs are among the most biodiverse marine ecosystems on the planet, hosting over 1,200 species of fish (10% of which are found nowhere else on Earth) and more than 200 species of hard and soft coral. The relatively isolated geography of the Red Sea has fostered unique evolution, producing endemic species such as the Red Sea clownfish, the masked pufferfish, and the striking royal angelfish. These reefs thrive in warm, clear waters with minimal river runoff and strong nutrient upwelling from the deep, creating conditions that support everything from microscopic nudibranchs to massive whale sharks. Marine scientists consider the Red Sea's reefs among the most resilient to climate change, making them critically important for global reef conservation research.

Ras Mohammed National Park

Located at the southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula where the Gulfs of Suez and Aqaba converge, Ras Mohammed was established as Egypt's first national park in 1983. The park protects spectacular underwater environments including the famous Shark Reef and Yolanda Reef, where sheer coral walls drop hundreds of meters into the deep blue and massive schools of barracuda, trevally, and snapper swirl in the current. Above water, the park encompasses mangrove forests, salt marshes, and desert landscapes that support populations of Nubian ibex, Dorcas gazelle, and numerous reptile species. The mangrove channel at the park's interior is one of the northernmost mangrove stands in the world, providing critical nursery habitat for juvenile reef fish and migratory birds.

White Desert Geological Wonders

The White Desert (Sahara el-Beyda) near Farafra Oasis is one of Egypt's most surreal natural landscapes. Millions of years ago, this area lay beneath a shallow tropical sea, and the chalk and limestone formations left behind have been sculpted by wind erosion into an otherworldly gallery of natural sculptures -- mushroom-shaped hoodoos, towering pillars, balanced rocks, and formations that resemble animals, faces, and abstract art. The brilliant white chalk glows pink and gold at sunrise and sunset, and under a full moon the landscape takes on an almost lunar quality. Interspersed among the chalk formations are crystal-studded geodes and fossilized seashells, tangible reminders that this bone-dry desert was once an ocean floor. The adjacent Black Desert, with its dark volcanic dolerite hills, provides a dramatic color contrast just a short drive away.

Wadi El Rayan & Fayoum

The Fayoum Oasis, located about 100 kilometers southwest of Cairo, is one of Egypt's most accessible natural escapes and a region of remarkable ecological and paleontological significance. Wadi El Rayan, a protected area within Fayoum, contains Egypt's only waterfalls -- a series of cascades created by agricultural runoff channeled into the desert depression, forming two large artificial lakes that now support thriving bird populations and recreational areas. Nearby, the Valley of the Whales (Wadi Al-Hitan), a UNESCO World Heritage Site, preserves the fossilized remains of ancient whale ancestors that lived here 40 million years ago when the Sahara was a shallow ocean. The fossils document the evolutionary transition of whales from land-dwelling to ocean-going creatures, making it one of the most important paleontological sites in the world. Lake Qarun, a remnant of the ancient Lake Moeris, attracts flamingos and migratory waterfowl.

Nile Birdlife

The Nile River corridor and its associated wetlands serve as a vital flyway for millions of migratory birds traveling between Europe, Asia, and sub-Saharan Africa. Over 480 bird species have been recorded in Egypt, making it a significant destination for birdwatching enthusiasts. Along the Nile, resident species include the graceful cattle egret, the grey heron, the pied kingfisher hovering above the water before its dramatic plunge, and the striking hoopoe -- Egypt's national bird, revered since pharaonic times and depicted in ancient tomb paintings. The Nile Delta's wetlands and coastal lagoons attract vast flocks of flamingos, pelicans, spoonbills, and shorebirds during migration seasons. Lake Nasser, the enormous reservoir behind the Aswan High Dam, has developed its own thriving ecosystem and is home to Nile crocodiles, soft-shelled turtles, and colonies of cormorants and African fish eagles.

Desert Wildlife

Despite the harsh conditions, Egypt's deserts support a surprisingly diverse array of adapted wildlife. The Dorcas gazelle, the smallest gazelle species, roams the desert fringes in small herds, while the elegant Ruppell's fox and the fennec fox -- the world's smallest canid, with its enormous heat-radiating ears -- hunt at night across the sand seas. The Nubian ibex, a sure-footed mountain goat, navigates the rocky cliffs of the Sinai and Eastern Desert with remarkable agility. Overhead, golden eagles, Bonelli's eagles, and Egyptian vultures patrol the thermals, while the desert skies at night come alive with bats hunting insects. Reptiles thrive in the arid environment, including the Egyptian cobra (the sacred uraeus of the pharaohs), monitor lizards, and the striking Sinai agama with its brilliant blue and orange coloration. Conservation programs are working to protect these species and their fragile desert habitats from development and climate pressures.

Festivals & Events

Egypt's calendar brims with celebrations that reflect its layered heritage -- from ancient sun alignments to Islamic holy months, Coptic traditions, and contemporary cultural festivals.

☀️

Abu Simbel Sun Festival

Twice each year, on February 22 and October 22, the rising sun performs an extraordinary feat of ancient engineering at Abu Simbel. A shaft of sunlight penetrates 60 meters through the temple's entrance corridor to illuminate the inner sanctuary, bathing three of the four seated statues of gods in brilliant light while the statue of Ptah, god of the underworld, remains deliberately in shadow. These dates are believed to mark the birthday and coronation of Ramesses II, demonstrating the pharaonic astronomers' extraordinary precision over 3,200 years ago. Thousands of visitors and local celebrants gather before dawn to witness this celestial spectacle, accompanied by traditional Nubian music, dance performances, and cultural festivities that transform the remote desert site into a jubilant open-air celebration.

🎬

Cairo International Film Festival

Founded in 1976, the Cairo International Film Festival (CIFF) is the oldest and most prestigious film festival in the Middle East and Africa, and one of only a handful recognized by the International Federation of Film Producers Associations. Held annually in November or December at the Cairo Opera House and various venues across the city, CIFF screens over 150 films from dozens of countries, spanning narrative features, documentaries, shorts, and experimental cinema. The festival serves as a vital platform for Arab and African filmmakers, with dedicated competition categories spotlighting emerging regional talent. Red carpet galas, director masterclasses, industry panels, and public screenings create a vibrant atmosphere that draws cinephiles, industry professionals, and international media to the Egyptian capital.

🌙

Ramadan in Cairo

Experiencing Ramadan in Cairo is one of the most immersive cultural encounters in the Muslim world. During this holy month, the city undergoes a remarkable transformation: streets are adorned with colorful lanterns (fanous), mosques overflow with worshippers, and the rhythm of daily life shifts dramatically as millions fast from dawn to dusk. The magic intensifies at iftar (the sunset meal breaking the fast), when the entire city pauses simultaneously, families gather around lavish spreads, and restaurants offer special Ramadan menus featuring traditional dishes like qamar al-din (apricot juice), sambousak, and fattah. After iftar, Cairo comes alive with a festive energy unlike any other time of year -- streets buzz with activity well past midnight, Sufi music performances echo from mosques, and special Ramadan tents (khiyam) serve food, sweets, and tea to passersby. The suhoor (pre-dawn meal) tradition, accompanied by the rhythmic beating of the mesaharati (dawn drummer), adds another layer of atmospheric magic.

🎉

Moulid Celebrations

Moulids are vibrant Egyptian folk festivals celebrating the birthdays of Prophet Muhammad, Muslim saints, and other holy figures. These uniquely Egyptian celebrations blend Islamic devotion with ancient folk traditions, creating spectacles of sound, color, and communal joy. The largest is the Moulid al-Nabi (Prophet Muhammad's birthday), observed nationwide with processions, sweet shops selling special candy dolls (arouset el-moulid) and sugar horses, and illuminated streets. The Moulid of Sayyid al-Badawi in Tanta draws over two million pilgrims to the Nile Delta city, making it one of the largest religious gatherings in the Muslim world. Moulids feature Sufi dhikr circles with hypnotic chanting and rhythmic swaying, carnival rides, street food stalls, folk musicians, and an atmosphere of ecstatic spiritual celebration that is unmistakably Egyptian.

🌸

Sham el-Nessim

Sham el-Nessim ("Smelling the Breeze") is one of Egypt's oldest surviving celebrations, with origins tracing back over 4,500 years to ancient pharaonic spring festivals. Celebrated the day after Coptic Easter Monday, this secular holiday is observed by all Egyptians regardless of religion, making it one of the country's most unifying traditions. Families head outdoors to parks, gardens, the Nile banks, and the countryside for picnics featuring traditional foods: fesikh (salted, fermented grey mullet), renga (smoked herring), spring onions, lettuce, and colored eggs. The festival marks the arrival of spring and the renewal of life along the Nile, echoing the ancient Egyptian celebrations of Shemu, the harvest season. Public gardens in Cairo, Alexandria, and other cities overflow with families enjoying the warm weather, flying kites, and celebrating the arrival of the new season.

⭐

Coptic Christmas

Egyptian Coptic Christians -- who make up approximately 10-15% of the population -- celebrate Christmas on January 7 following the Julian calendar. The celebration begins on Christmas Eve (January 6) with a midnight mass that can last until the early morning hours, attended by millions across the country. The Cathedral of the Nativity in Egypt's new administrative capital, one of the largest churches in the Middle East, hosts the main national service attended by the Coptic Pope and broadcast live on television. Traditional Coptic Christmas foods include fatta (a layered dish of rice, bread, and meat in garlic-vinegar sauce) and kahk (butter cookies). The celebration carries special significance in Egypt, where the Coptic Church -- one of the oldest Christian churches in the world, founded by Saint Mark in the 1st century AD -- has maintained an unbroken tradition for nearly two thousand years.

El Gouna Film Festival

Launched in 2017 in the purpose-built Red Sea resort town of El Gouna, this rapidly growing film festival has become one of the most glamorous cultural events in the Middle East. Held annually in October, GFF combines serious cinema with a beachside setting, attracting international stars, acclaimed directors, and industry professionals. The festival's program features world premieres, regional debuts, and retrospectives, with a strong focus on Arab and African cinema alongside international selections. Cash prizes and grants support emerging filmmakers, while workshops, panels, and pitch forums provide professional development opportunities. The festival's unique setting -- screenings take place in open-air venues along the marina and in purpose-built cinemas -- combined with El Gouna's luxury hotels and Red Sea backdrop create an atmosphere that blends Hollywood glamour with Egyptian hospitality.

Sound & Light Shows at the Pyramids

The Sound and Light Show at the Pyramids of Giza has been dazzling audiences since 1961, making it one of the longest-running spectacles in the world. As darkness falls over the Giza plateau, the Great Pyramids and the Sphinx are illuminated with dramatic colored lights while a narrated story of ancient Egypt unfolds through powerful speakers, recounting the tales of the pharaohs, the construction of the pyramids, and the rise and fall of dynasties. The Sphinx itself serves as the narrator in some versions, its ancient stone face lit against the night sky as laser projections dance across the monument faces. Shows are offered in multiple languages including English, Arabic, French, Spanish, and German, and the experience of watching the pyramids emerge from darkness under colored light is genuinely magical. Similar sound and light shows are also held at Karnak Temple in Luxor and Philae Temple in Aswan, each with their own unique narratives drawn from the mythology of the ancient sites.

Practical Travel Information

Essential details to help you plan your Egyptian adventure.

Visa Requirements

Most nationalities can obtain a tourist visa on arrival at Egyptian airports (approximately $25 USD) or apply for an e-visa online before travel. The standard tourist visa allows a 30-day stay. Some nationalities require advance embassy approval. Always verify the latest requirements with the Egyptian embassy in your country before departure.

Currency

The Egyptian Pound (EGP) is the official currency. ATMs are widely available in cities and tourist areas. US dollars and euros are widely accepted at hotels and tourist shops, but Egyptian pounds are needed for local markets, transport, and smaller establishments. Credit cards are accepted at most hotels and upscale restaurants.

Language

Arabic is the official language, with Egyptian Arabic being the most widely spoken dialect. English is commonly spoken in tourist areas, hotels, and by guides. French is also understood in some areas. Learning a few Arabic phrases such as "shukran" (thank you) and "as-salamu alaykum" (peace be upon you) is appreciated by locals.

Best Time to Visit

October to April offers the most comfortable weather for sightseeing, with mild temperatures and minimal rainfall. December to February is peak season with the best conditions but larger crowds. Summer (May to September) brings intense heat, especially in Upper Egypt, but the Red Sea coast remains pleasant year-round for beach and diving holidays.

Getting Around

Domestic flights connect Cairo with Luxor, Aswan, Hurghada, and Sharm el-Sheikh efficiently. Trains run along the Nile Valley between Cairo, Luxor, and Aswan. Within cities, ride-hailing apps like Uber and Careem operate in Cairo and Alexandria. For the most immersive experience, Nile cruises combine transport with sightseeing.

Health & Safety

No mandatory vaccinations are required for Egypt, but hepatitis A, typhoid, and routine vaccinations are recommended. Drink bottled water and use sun protection. Tourist areas are well-policed and generally safe. Travel insurance covering medical evacuation is strongly recommended. The emergency number in Egypt is 122 for police and 123 for ambulance.

Accommodation Guide

Egypt offers an exceptional range of accommodation options, from opulent five-star properties with pyramid views to authentic desert camps under Saharan skies.

Luxury Hotels & Nile-View Properties

Egypt boasts some of the most iconic luxury hotels in the world. The historic Marriott Mena House, with its unobstructed views of the Great Pyramids from its gardens and pool, has hosted kings, presidents, and celebrities since 1886. The Sofitel Old Cataract in Aswan, perched on a granite cliff overlooking the Nile, is where Agatha Christie wrote "Death on the Nile." Cairo's Nile-front five-star properties -- including the Four Seasons, Kempinski, and Fairmont -- offer world-class dining, rooftop bars, and spa facilities. In Luxor, the Winter Palace and the Hilton provide elegant bases for exploring the temples. Red Sea resorts like the Oberoi Sahl Hasheesh and the Four Seasons Sharm el-Sheikh deliver beachfront luxury with private reefs. Expect to pay $150-500+ per night for these premium experiences, with suites and peak season rates climbing higher.

Nile Cruise Ships

Floating hotels that cruise between Luxor and Aswan are one of Egypt's most distinctive accommodation experiences. These multi-deck vessels range from standard three-star ships to ultra-luxury boutique cruises, with most journeys lasting 3-7 nights. Standard cruises ($200-400 per person for a 4-night trip) include comfortable cabins with river views, buffet dining, a sun deck with pool, and guided excursions to temples at Edfu, Kom Ombo, and other stops along the route. Premium vessels like the Oberoi Philae and Sanctuary Sun Boat offer suite accommodations with private balconies, gourmet dining, and personalized excursions ($800-2,000+ per person). For a more intimate experience, traditional dahabiya sailboats -- elegant two-masted vessels carrying just 10-20 passengers -- offer wind-powered journeys with a slower pace and stops at lesser-visited sites inaccessible to larger ships.

Budget Hostels & Guesthouses

Budget travelers will find Egypt remarkably affordable, with an extensive network of hostels, guesthouses, and budget hotels offering clean, comfortable accommodation at very low prices. Cairo's downtown area around Tahrir Square has dozens of backpacker-friendly hostels with dormitory beds from $5-10 per night and private rooms from $15-30. Luxor's West Bank has charming family-run guesthouses with rooftop terraces overlooking the Nile and the Theban Hills for $10-25 per night. Aswan's Nubian villages on the west bank offer simple but atmospheric homestay experiences. Dahab on the Sinai coast is legendary among budget travelers for its beachfront camps and guesthouses starting at $8-15 per night. In all price ranges, Egyptian hospitality shines through, and many budget accommodations offer home-cooked breakfast and invaluable local travel advice from their owners.

Desert Camps & Eco-Lodges

For travelers seeking immersion in Egypt's dramatic desert landscapes, a growing number of desert camps and eco-lodges offer unique overnight experiences far from conventional tourism. In the White Desert near Farafra, guided camping trips provide Bedouin-style meals cooked over open fires, sleeping bags under a sky blazing with stars, and sunrise walks among the chalk formations -- all for around $40-80 per person per night including meals and transport from the oasis. The Siwa Oasis has pioneered sustainable tourism with eco-lodges like Adrere Amellal and Taziry, which are built entirely from local materials (salt rock, mud brick, and palm wood) with no electricity by design, using candles and oil lamps to create an atmosphere of timeless tranquility. These lodges range from $100-350 per night and offer organic food from their own gardens, natural spring-fed pools, and guided desert excursions. In the Sinai, Bedouin camps near St. Catherine's offer simple mat-and-blanket accommodation under goat-hair tents for as little as $15-30 per night, including tea, bread, and stories around the campfire.

Shopping & Souvenirs

Egypt's markets and workshops offer a treasure trove of handcrafted goods, artistic traditions, and aromatic delights -- each purchase a tangible connection to the country's rich cultural heritage.

Papyrus Art

Authentic Egyptian papyrus makes a beautiful and historically meaningful souvenir. Real papyrus is made from the cyperus plant using techniques that closely follow the ancient Egyptian process -- stalks are sliced, soaked, layered crosswise, pressed, and dried to create a flexible, durable writing surface. Quality papyrus paintings depict scenes from pharaonic tomb art, hieroglyphic texts, or contemporary Egyptian motifs, and range from small bookmarks ($2-5) to large framed pieces ($30-200+). Be wary of cheap banana-leaf imitations sold at tourist sites; genuine papyrus has a distinctive woven texture and will bend without cracking. The best places to buy authenticated papyrus are dedicated papyrus institutes and galleries in Cairo, Luxor, and Aswan, where you can watch artisans demonstrate the ancient manufacturing process before making your purchase.

Alabaster & Stone Carvings

The alabaster workshops near the Valley of the Kings in Luxor have been carving this translucent stone for generations, producing everything from miniature canopic jars and scarab beetles to full-size replicas of pharaonic busts and vases. Egyptian alabaster (actually calcite) has been quarried from the same sites near Hatnub since the Old Kingdom, and its milky, luminous quality makes it instantly recognizable. Skilled artisans shape the stone on manual lathes and polish it to a smooth finish, creating pieces that range from affordable pocket-sized souvenirs ($5-15) to substantial decorative items ($50-300). Other popular stone carvings include basalt Anubis figures, granite obelisks, and carved limestone replicas of famous reliefs. Watching the craftsmen at work in the family workshops near the Colossi of Memnon is a fascinating experience in itself, and buying directly supports these traditional artisan communities.

Spices from Khan el-Khalili

Cairo's legendary Khan el-Khalili bazaar is a sensory wonderland for spice lovers, with aromatic stalls piled high with colorful pyramids of cumin, coriander, saffron, cardamom, dried hibiscus flowers, and dozens of spice blends. Egyptian spice merchants have perfected the art of blending, and popular mixes include dukkah (a nut-and-spice blend for dipping bread), baharat (a warm spice mix for meats), and karkade (dried hibiscus for making the beloved Egyptian tea). Dried herbs like dried mint, chamomile, and anise are also popular purchases. Prices are extremely affordable -- a generous bag of high-quality spices typically costs $2-8 -- but bargaining is expected and part of the fun. The spice sellers are usually happy to let you smell and taste before buying, and many will custom-blend mixtures to your preferences. Pack spices in sealed bags inside your checked luggage, and check customs regulations for your home country before purchasing large quantities.

Cotton & Textiles

Egyptian cotton is renowned worldwide for its exceptional quality -- the extra-long staple fibers produce fabric that is softer, stronger, and more lustrous than ordinary cotton. Buying Egyptian cotton products at their source offers significant savings over international retail prices. High-quality cotton bed linens, towels, bathrobes, and clothing are available at textile shops throughout Cairo (particularly in the downtown commercial district and Zamalek neighborhood) and in shopping malls. Traditional textiles like hand-loomed scarves, embroidered tablecloths, and applique work (khayamiya) from the Tentmakers' Bazaar (Shari'a al-Khayamiyya) near Bab Zuweila in Cairo are stunning one-of-a-kind souvenirs. The tentmakers produce vibrant, intricately stitched fabric panels using a technique dating back to the Fatimid era, with designs ranging from geometric Islamic patterns to pharaonic motifs and contemporary art. Prices range from $10 for small cushion covers to $200+ for large decorative panels.

Gold & Silver Jewelry

Egypt has a rich tradition of goldsmithing dating back to the pharaohs, and today's jewelry artisans continue to create pieces inspired by ancient Egyptian, Islamic geometric, and contemporary designs. The gold district in Khan el-Khalili and specialized jewelry shops throughout Cairo offer cartouche pendants -- oval frames engraved with your name in hieroglyphics -- that are among the most popular souvenirs from Egypt. Gold is sold by weight and current market prices, making Egyptian gold jewelry excellent value. The standard purity is 18-karat or 21-karat. Silver jewelry featuring pharaonic symbols like the Eye of Horus, ankh (key of life), and scarab beetle are more affordable options, ranging from $5-50 depending on complexity and weight. Nubian-style silver jewelry from Aswan features distinctive tribal designs with turquoise and coral accents. Always purchase from reputable shops that provide hallmark stamps and certificates of authenticity.

Perfume & Essential Oils

Egypt has been a center of perfume production since the pharaonic era, when fragrant oils were used in religious rituals, embalming, and daily grooming. Today, Egyptian perfumeries -- particularly in Aswan and Luxor -- offer an extraordinary range of pure essential oils and perfume concentrates without the alcohol or chemical additives found in commercial fragrances. Popular Egyptian essences include lotus (the sacred flower of ancient Egypt), jasmine, rose, sandalwood, amber, musk, and papyrus. Perfume shops typically offer an interactive experience where the proprietor guides you through dozens of scents, explains the extraction process, and creates custom blends to your taste. Prices vary widely depending on the oil type and concentration -- pure lotus or rose otto oils can be expensive ($30-100 for a small bottle), while blended fragrances and lighter essences are very affordable ($5-20). The ornate hand-blown glass perfume bottles made in Cairo's glass workshops are beautiful souvenirs in their own right, available from $3-30 depending on size and intricacy.

Culture & Etiquette

Understanding Egyptian culture enriches your travel experience and shows respect for your hosts.

Egypt is a predominantly Muslim country with a significant Coptic Christian minority, and religion plays an important role in daily life. Visitors are welcomed warmly, and Egyptians are renowned for their hospitality and friendliness. The Arabic greeting "ahlan wa sahlan" (welcome) reflects the genuine openness you will encounter throughout the country.

When visiting mosques and religious sites, dress modestly by covering shoulders and knees. Women may be asked to cover their hair in some mosques, so carrying a scarf is practical. During Ramadan, the holy month of fasting, be considerate by avoiding eating, drinking, or smoking in public during daylight hours. Ramadan evenings, however, are some of the most festive and communal times to experience Egyptian culture.

Egyptians are proud of their heritage, which encompasses pharaonic, Coptic, Islamic, and modern influences. Engaging with this layered history respectfully and showing genuine interest will open doors to meaningful cultural exchanges. Tipping (baksheesh) is customary and an important part of the service economy. Small tips for guides, drivers, hotel staff, and restaurant servers are expected and appreciated.

Egyptian cuisine is flavorful and diverse. National dishes include koshari (a hearty mix of rice, lentils, pasta, and spiced tomato sauce), ful medames (stewed fava beans), and ta'ameya (Egyptian falafel made from fava beans rather than chickpeas). Sharing a meal is central to Egyptian social life, and food is prepared with pride and generosity.

Music is woven into the fabric of Egyptian life, from ancient times to the present day. The legendary Oum Kalthoum, often called "The Star of the East," remains the most revered singer in the Arab world decades after her death in 1975 -- her monthly radio concerts in the mid-20th century brought the entire Arab world to a standstill, and her emotional, hour-long performances of classical Arabic poetry set to orchestral music continue to be played in taxis, cafes, and homes across Egypt. Classical Arabic music traditions persist alongside the contemporary phenomenon of mahraganat (festival music), an energetic electronic street genre born in Cairo's working-class neighborhoods that has taken the youth culture by storm with its auto-tuned vocals, rapid-fire lyrics, and infectious beats. In the south, Nubian music offers a completely different sonic landscape -- its pentatonic scales, call-and-response vocals, hand-drumming, and distinctive dances reflect the heritage of the Nile Valley's indigenous people. Live music is everywhere in Egypt, from Sufi dhikr ceremonies and wedding processions to downtown jazz clubs and Red Sea resort stages.

Egypt's architectural heritage is among the richest and most diverse in the world, spanning at least five thousand years of continuous building tradition. Pharaonic architecture -- the pyramids, temples, and rock-cut tombs -- set standards of monumental scale and geometric precision that influenced builders for millennia. The massive pylons and hypostyle halls of Karnak and Luxor Temple demonstrate an understanding of structural engineering, astronomical alignment, and artistic decoration that remains impressive by modern standards. Coptic architecture, emerging in the early centuries of Christianity, adapted Roman basilica forms with distinctive Egyptian elements -- ornate woodwork, inlaid ivory screens (mashrabiya), and wall paintings in vivid natural pigments. Cairo's Hanging Church (Al-Mu'allaqa) and the monasteries of Wadi Natrun preserve this tradition beautifully. Islamic architecture reached extraordinary heights in Cairo, where the Fatimid, Ayyubid, and Mamluk dynasties competed to build ever more elaborate mosques, madrasas, and mausoleums. The Mamluk period in particular produced masterworks of stone carving, stained glass, marble inlay, and muqarnas (honeycomb vaulting) that rank among the finest achievements of Islamic art. Walking through Islamic Cairo's Al-Muizz Street is like strolling through an open-air museum of architectural evolution spanning five centuries of creative ambition.

Frequently Asked Questions About Traveling to Egypt

Answers to the most common questions travelers have about visiting Egypt.

The top destinations in Egypt include Cairo and the Pyramids of Giza, Luxor with its Valley of the Kings and Karnak Temple, Aswan and its Nubian villages, the Red Sea resort towns of Hurghada and Sharm el-Sheikh for world-class diving, historic Alexandria on the Mediterranean coast, and the magnificent Abu Simbel temples near the Sudanese border. A Nile cruise between Luxor and Aswan is one of the most iconic travel experiences in the world.

The best time to visit Egypt is from October to April when temperatures are milder and comfortable for sightseeing. Winter months (December to February) are the peak tourist season with pleasant daytime temperatures of 20-25°C (68-77°F). Summer (May to September) can be extremely hot, especially in Upper Egypt where temperatures often exceed 40°C (104°F). The Red Sea coast is enjoyable year-round, with warm waters ideal for diving and snorkeling.

Most visitors to Egypt need a visa. Citizens of many countries, including the US, UK, EU nations, and Australia, can obtain a visa on arrival at Egyptian airports for around $25 USD, or apply for an e-visa online before travel. The standard tourist visa is valid for 30 days. Some nationalities require advance embassy approval. Always check the latest requirements with your nearest Egyptian embassy before traveling.

Egypt is generally safe for tourists, particularly in major tourist areas like Cairo, Luxor, Aswan, and Red Sea resorts. The Egyptian government places high priority on tourist safety, and popular sites have dedicated tourist police. As with any destination, exercise standard precautions: stay aware of your surroundings, use licensed guides and reputable tour operators, keep valuables secure, and follow local advice. Most visits to Egypt are trouble-free and rewarding.

Egypt is an affordable destination by international standards. Budget travelers can manage on $30-50 USD per day, covering basic accommodation, local food, and transport. Mid-range travelers should budget $80-150 USD per day for comfortable hotels, guided tours, and restaurant meals. A multi-day Nile cruise ranges from $200-800+ per person depending on the vessel class. Luxury travelers can expect to spend $300-1,000+ per day. Entry fees to major sites like the Pyramids cost $5-20 each.

No vaccinations are mandatory for entering Egypt unless you are arriving from a yellow fever endemic country, in which case proof of yellow fever vaccination is required. However, health authorities recommend being up to date on routine vaccinations including measles-mumps-rubella (MMR), diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis, varicella, and polio. Additionally, hepatitis A and typhoid vaccinations are recommended for most travelers. Hepatitis B and rabies vaccines may be advised for longer stays or rural travel. Consult your doctor or a travel health clinic at least 4-6 weeks before departure.

Yes, women can and do travel solo in Egypt, though some extra awareness and preparation can enhance the experience. Dress modestly by covering shoulders and knees, which aligns with local customs and reduces unwanted attention. Stick to well-traveled tourist areas, use reputable tour operators, and consider joining group tours for excursions. Many hotels and Nile cruises cater specifically to solo travelers. Ride-hailing apps like Uber and Careem are safer than hailing taxis on the street. Thousands of solo female travelers visit Egypt successfully every year.

Tipping (baksheesh) is an integral part of Egyptian culture and economy. For restaurant meals, 10-15% is standard if service charge is not included. Hotel porters expect 20-50 EGP per bag, housekeeping 20-50 EGP per day. Tour guides typically receive 100-200 EGP per person per day, and drivers about half that amount. For small services like bathroom attendants or photo opportunities at monuments, 5-10 EGP is appropriate. Felucca captains, Nile cruise staff, and spa attendants also expect tips. Always carry small bills in Egyptian pounds for tipping.

Egypt is one of the most affordable travel destinations in North Africa and the Middle East. Local food, transport, and accommodation offer excellent value. A filling street meal of koshari or ful costs under $1 USD, while a sit-down restaurant meal runs $5-15 USD. Budget hotels start from $15-30 per night, mid-range hotels $50-120, and luxury properties $150-500+. Domestic flights are reasonably priced at $50-120 one way. The main expenses are guided tours and entrance fees to archaeological sites, but even these are modest by international standards.

Egypt is a conservative country, so modest dress is recommended, especially outside resort areas. For men, long trousers or knee-length shorts and t-shirts are fine. Women should cover shoulders and knees in public areas, and carry a scarf for visiting mosques. Lightweight, breathable fabrics in natural fibers like cotton and linen are ideal for the heat. Comfortable walking shoes are essential for archaeological sites. At Red Sea resorts, beachwear is acceptable at pools and beaches. Bring layers for air-conditioned museums and cool desert evenings. A hat and sunglasses are must-haves year-round.
