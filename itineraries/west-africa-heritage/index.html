Day-by-Day Itinerary

Your complete 14-day West Africa heritage journey broken down day by day.

Days 1–2: Accra, Ghana

Day 1: Arrive at Kotoka International Airport and transfer to your hotel in the Osu or Labone district. After settling in, explore Jamestown, Accra's oldest neighborhood with its lighthouse, fishing harbor, and vibrant street life. Visit the Kwame Nkrumah Mausoleum and Memorial Park, honoring Ghana's founding father and pan-African visionary. Evening welcome dinner featuring Ghanaian specialties: jollof rice, grilled tilapia, and kelewele (spiced fried plantain).

Day 2: Visit the National Museum of Ghana for an overview of the country's history from ancient kingdoms to independence. Explore the bustling Makola Market, Accra's largest open-air market overflowing with textiles, beads, spices, and everything imaginable. Afternoon visit to the W.E.B. Du Bois Centre, the final home of the African American scholar who became a Ghanaian citizen. In the evening, experience Accra's vibrant nightlife with live highlife music and Afrobeats at one of the city's clubs.

Accommodation: Accra hotel or guesthouse · Meals: Dinner (Day 1), Breakfast (Day 2)

Days 3–4: Cape Coast

Day 3: Drive west along the coast to Cape Coast (approximately 3 hours). Visit the UNESCO-listed Cape Coast Castle, a sobering and essential experience. Walk through the dark dungeons where enslaved people were held, pass through the Door of No Return, and reflect on this chapter of human history in the castle museum. The guided tour provides powerful historical context and emotional depth.

Day 4: Morning visit to Elmina Castle, the oldest European structure in sub-Saharan Africa, built by the Portuguese in 1482. After the castle, adventure into Kakum National Park for the famous canopy walkway, a series of seven suspension bridges 40 meters above the rainforest floor. Spot hornbills, butterflies, and if lucky, forest elephants from this unique aerial perspective. Visit a traditional fishing village along the coast and learn about the local fishing traditions that have sustained communities for centuries.

Accommodation: Cape Coast guesthouse or beach lodge · Meals: Breakfast daily

Days 5–6: Kumasi

Day 5: Drive inland to Kumasi, the historic capital of the Ashanti Empire and Ghana's second-largest city. Visit Manhyia Palace Museum, the seat of the Asantehene (Ashanti king), and learn about the rich traditions of one of Africa's most powerful pre-colonial kingdoms. Explore the Prempeh II Jubilee Museum housing Ashanti royal regalia, golden artifacts, and historical displays. The Ashanti Kingdom's sophisticated governance, artistry, and military prowess rival any civilization in history.

Day 6: Immerse yourself in Kumasi's creative heritage. Visit Kejetia Market, the largest single-structure market in West Africa, a labyrinth of over 10,000 stalls. In the nearby village of Bonwire, watch master weavers produce kente cloth on traditional looms, each pattern telling a story or conveying a proverb. Visit an adinkra stamping workshop to learn about these Ashanti symbols of wisdom. Afternoon visit to Kumasi Fort, a relic of the Anglo-Ashanti Wars, housing the Armed Forces Museum.

Accommodation: Kumasi hotel · Meals: Breakfast daily

Days 7–8: Mole National Park

Day 7: Early departure for the long drive north to Mole National Park (approximately 6 hours through the savanna). Mole is Ghana's largest wildlife reserve, protecting 4,577 square kilometers of Guinea savanna woodland. Check in to the cliff-top lodge overlooking two waterholes frequented by elephants and arrive in time for an afternoon game drive. Spot elephants, warthogs, baboons, kob antelopes, and an array of birdlife against the backdrop of golden savanna.

Day 8: The highlight of Mole is the walking safari, a rare and thrilling way to experience African wildlife on foot. Led by armed rangers, walk through the bush tracking elephants, hartebeest, and bushbuck while learning to read animal signs, identify medicinal plants, and understand the ecosystem. The proximity to wild elephants on foot is breathtaking. Afternoon visit to the Larabanga Mosque, one of West Africa's oldest mosques (c. 1421), a striking example of Sudano-Sahelian architecture. Begin the journey south or fly from Tamale to Accra, then connect to Dakar.

Accommodation: Mole Motel or lodge · Meals: All meals included

Days 9–10: Dakar, Senegal

Day 9: Arrive in Dakar, Senegal's vibrant capital perched on the Cap-Vert peninsula, the westernmost point of mainland Africa. Transfer to your hotel in the Plateau or Almadies district. Visit the African Renaissance Monument, a 49-meter bronze statue overlooking the Atlantic, then explore the IFAN Museum of African Arts at Cheikh Anta Diop University, housing one of West Africa's finest collections of masks, textiles, and musical instruments. Evening stroll along the Corniche with its ocean views and lively restaurants.

Day 10: Dive deeper into Dakar's cultural riches. Visit the Village des Arts, a colony of artist studios in a former military camp. Explore the Kermel Market and Sandaga Market for Senegalese fabrics, jewelry, and crafts. Take a pirogue (traditional fishing boat) to Ngor Island for a swim on one of Dakar's most beautiful beaches. Enjoy a traditional Senegalese lunch of thieboudienne (rice and fish, the national dish) prepared by a local family. Evening visit to a live mbalax music venue to experience Senegal's infectious rhythms.

Accommodation: Dakar hotel or boutique guesthouse · Meals: Breakfast daily, lunch on Day 10

Day 11: Gorée Island

Take the 20-minute ferry from Dakar to Gorée Island, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of Africa's most historically significant places. This small, car-free island was a center of the transatlantic slave trade for over 300 years. Visit the House of Slaves (Maison des Esclaves) and stand at the Door of No Return, where enslaved Africans took their last steps on home soil. The island's pastel-colored colonial buildings, bougainvillea-draped streets, and art galleries create a hauntingly beautiful contrast with its painful history. Explore the Historical Museum and the Maritime Museum. Enjoy lunch at a seaside restaurant before returning to Dakar in the afternoon for personal reflection.

Accommodation: Dakar hotel · Meals: Breakfast & lunch

Days 12–13: Saint-Louis

Day 12: Drive north to Saint-Louis (approximately 4 hours), Senegal's former colonial capital and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The city occupies a narrow island in the Senegal River, connected by the iconic Faidherbe Bridge designed by Gustave Eiffel's workshop. Explore the island's faded colonial architecture, colorful horse-drawn carts, and atmospheric streets. Visit the Saint-Louis Museum and the fishing quarter of Guet Ndar, a dense neighborhood where hundreds of brightly painted pirogues launch daily into the Atlantic.

Day 13: Morning excursion to the Djoudj National Bird Sanctuary, a UNESCO site and the third-largest ornithological park in the world. Between November and April, over 3 million migratory birds stop here, including pelicans, flamingos, spoonbills, and rare species from Europe and Asia. Take a boat trip through the wetlands for close-up views of nesting colonies. Afternoon at leisure in Saint-Louis to explore the city's jazz heritage. Saint-Louis hosts Africa's premier jazz festival each May, and music fills the streets year-round.

Accommodation: Saint-Louis boutique hotel · Meals: Breakfast daily

Day 14: Lac Rose & Departure

Drive south from Saint-Louis to Lac Rose (Lac Retba), a stunning pink-hued lake colored by Dunaliella salga algae and high salt content (up to 40%, rivaling the Dead Sea). Watch salt harvesters wade into the rose-colored waters, their bodies coated in shea butter for protection, scooping salt from the lakebed as they have for generations. Float effortlessly in the dense brine. Visit a local village and enjoy a farewell lunch of Senegalese grilled fish. Transfer to Blaise Diagne International Airport for your departure flight, carrying the stories, rhythms, and resilient spirit of West Africa with you.

Accommodation: N/A (departure day) · Meals: Breakfast & lunch

Practical Information

Essential details for planning your West Africa heritage journey.

Getting There & Around

International flights arrive at Accra's Kotoka International Airport (ACC). The Ghana segment uses a combination of private vehicle and domestic flights (Accra-Tamale for Mole). A flight connects Accra to Dakar (approximately 3.5 hours). In Senegal, overland transfers by private vehicle cover all routes. Departure is from Dakar's Blaise Diagne International Airport (DSS). Roads in Ghana are generally good on main routes; Senegal's highways are excellent.

Money & Costs

Ghana uses the Cedi (GHS); Senegal uses the West African CFA Franc (XOF). ATMs are available in major cities but scarce in rural areas; bring sufficient cash for Mole and Saint-Louis. Budget travelers can manage on $50-80/day. Mid-range travel runs $100-180/day. Mobile money (MTN, Orange Money) is widely used. Tipping is appreciated: 10% in restaurants, small tips for guides and porters.

Health & Vaccinations

Yellow fever vaccination is mandatory for both Ghana and Senegal. Additional recommended vaccines: hepatitis A/B, typhoid, meningitis, and rabies. Malaria prophylaxis is essential. Drink only bottled or purified water. Bring insect repellent with DEET and long-sleeved clothing for evenings. Travel health insurance with medical evacuation coverage is strongly recommended. Pharmacies in major cities are well-stocked.

Visa & Entry

Ghana requires a visa for most nationalities, obtainable online or at embassies ($60-150 depending on nationality). Senegal allows visa-free entry for citizens of many countries including the US, UK, and EU for stays up to 90 days. Passports must be valid for 6+ months with at least 2 blank pages. Both countries require a yellow fever vaccination certificate. Print copies of all travel documents as backup.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the West Africa Heritage itinerary.

The best time to visit Ghana and Senegal is during the dry season from November to March. Temperatures are warm but not extreme, humidity is lower, and roads are in good condition. The Harmattan wind (December-February) can cause hazy skies in northern Ghana but does not significantly impact travel. The rainy season (May-October) makes some rural roads difficult, particularly access to Mole National Park. Senegal's rainy season peaks July-September. For wildlife viewing at Mole, the late dry season (February-March) is optimal as animals gather around shrinking water sources.

Yes, a yellow fever vaccination certificate is mandatory for entry into both Ghana and Senegal. Anti-malaria prophylaxis is strongly recommended for all areas. Consult a travel health clinic 6-8 weeks before departure. Bring insect repellent with DEET and sleep under mosquito nets. Tap water is not safe to drink; use bottled or purified water throughout your trip.

The most practical way to travel between Ghana and Senegal is by air. Direct flights between Accra and Dakar take approximately 3.5 hours and are operated by several West African airlines. Overland travel is possible but takes multiple days through Togo, Benin, Nigeria, and other countries, requiring multiple visas and border crossings. We recommend flying from Accra (or Tamale near Mole) to Dakar to maximize your time at each destination. Book flights in advance as routes can sell out during peak season.
