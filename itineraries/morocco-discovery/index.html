Day-by-Day Itinerary

Your complete 8-day Morocco adventure broken down day by day.

Day 1: Arrive in Marrakech

Arrive at Marrakech Menara Airport and transfer to your riad in the heart of the medina. Settle into this traditional Moroccan house with its interior courtyard, mosaic tiles, and rooftop terrace. In the afternoon, explore the Bahia Palace, a masterpiece of Moroccan architecture with its painted ceilings and lush gardens. As evening falls, head to Jemaa el-Fna, the pulsating heart of Marrakech. Watch snake charmers, acrobats, and Gnawa musicians while the air fills with smoke from dozens of food stalls. Sample grilled meats, snail soup, and freshly squeezed orange juice.

Accommodation: Traditional riad in the medina · Meals: Dinner

Day 2: Marrakech Exploration

A full day to discover Marrakech's riches. Morning visit to the Majorelle Garden, a botanical paradise created by French painter Jacques Majorelle and later restored by Yves Saint Laurent. The cobalt-blue villa houses the Berber Museum. Continue to the Saadian Tombs and Ben Youssef Madrasa, an exquisite 14th-century Islamic college. After lunch in the medina, dive into the souks: labyrinthine markets selling carpets, leather goods, ceramics, lanterns, and spices. Learn the art of haggling over mint tea with shopkeepers. In the late afternoon, enjoy a traditional hammam experience to relax before dinner on a rooftop terrace overlooking the medina.

Accommodation: Traditional riad in the medina · Meals: Breakfast

Day 3: Atlas Mountains

Leave the city behind and drive into the High Atlas Mountains, North Africa's tallest range. Wind through dramatic passes with breathtaking views of terraced valleys and red-earth Berber villages clinging to mountainsides. Stop in the Ourika Valley or Imlil, a gateway village to Mount Toubkal (4,167m), North Africa's highest peak. Trek through walnut groves and visit a traditional Berber home for a lunch of tagine cooked over open fire. Learn about Berber culture, their centuries-old agricultural traditions, and the significance of the Atlas Mountains as a cultural divide between the Sahara and the Atlantic coast. Return to Marrakech or continue to Ait Benhaddou.

Accommodation: Mountain guesthouse or kasbah · Meals: Breakfast & lunch

Days 4–5: Sahara Desert & Merzouga

Day 4 brings the dramatic drive across the High Atlas via the Tizi n'Tichka Pass (2,260m), stopping at the UNESCO-listed ksar of Ait Benhaddou, a fortified village used as a backdrop in numerous films including Gladiator and Game of Thrones. Continue through the Valley of Roses and Dades Gorge to reach Merzouga at the edge of Erg Chebbi. In the late afternoon, mount camels for a trek into the towering sand dunes, reaching your desert camp as the sun paints the Sahara in fiery colors. Enjoy a traditional Berber dinner with drumming under a sky ablaze with stars.

Day 5 begins with a desert sunrise from the top of the dunes, a transcendent experience of silence and light. After breakfast at camp, return to Merzouga and begin the journey north toward Fez through the Ziz Valley, passing palm oases, crumbling kasbahs, and the dramatic Ziz Gorge. Stop in Midelt or Ifrane along the way.

Accommodation: Desert camp (Day 4), hotel en route (Day 5) · Meals: All meals included

Days 6–7: Fez

Arrive in Fez, Morocco's spiritual and intellectual capital. Day 6 is dedicated to Fez el-Bali, the world's largest car-free urban area. With a licensed guide, navigate the medina's 9,000 winding alleys, discovering the ancient Chouara Tanneries where leather is still dyed in stone vats using medieval techniques, the Al Quaraouiyine University (founded in 859 AD, the world's oldest existing university), and the ornate Bou Inania Madrasa. Visit artisan workshops producing ceramics, zellige tilework, and brass lanterns.

Day 7 explores the Mellah (Jewish quarter) and the Royal Palace gates before venturing to the Merenid Tombs for a panoramic view over the entire medina. Afternoon visit to a traditional Fassi cooking class where you learn to prepare pastilla, harira, and Moroccan salads. Evening stroll through the Ville Nouvelle for a different perspective on this multifaceted city.

Accommodation: Riad in Fez medina · Meals: Breakfast, lunch on Day 7

Day 8: Chefchaouen & Departure

Early morning drive northwest through rolling hills and olive groves to Chefchaouen, the legendary Blue City. Nestled in the Rif Mountains at 660 meters, the entire old town is washed in shades of blue, from powder to cobalt to indigo. Explore the compact medina, visit the 15th-century kasbah and its ethnographic museum, and photograph the endlessly photogenic blue-washed lanes. Browse shops selling woven blankets, goat cheese, and the region's distinctive round bread. Enjoy a final Moroccan meal overlooking the town before transferring to Tangier for departure, carrying the colors, flavors, and warmth of Morocco with you.

Accommodation: N/A (departure day) · Meals: Breakfast & lunch

Practical Information

Essential details for planning your Morocco discovery trip.

Getting There & Around

International flights arrive at Marrakech Menara Airport (RAK). Departure is typically from Tangier Ibn Battouta Airport (TNG) or Fez-Saiss Airport (FEZ). Overland travel by private vehicle or shared minibus covers all transfers between cities. Roads are generally good, though mountain passes require experienced drivers. Distances are significant: Marrakech to Merzouga is approximately 9 hours, while Fez to Chefchaouen is about 4 hours.

Money & Costs

Morocco's currency is the Dirham (MAD). ATMs are widely available in cities. Budget travelers can manage on $50-80/day with guesthouses and street food. Mid-range riads cost $60-150/night. Credit cards are accepted at larger establishments but cash is essential in souks and rural areas. Tipping is customary: 10-15% in restaurants, 20-50 MAD for guides, and small tips for porters and riad staff.

Culture & Etiquette

Morocco is an Islamic country with conservative customs. Dress modestly, especially when visiting mosques (non-Muslims cannot enter most mosques except Hassan II in Casablanca). Remove shoes before entering homes and riads. Use your right hand for eating and greetings. During Ramadan, avoid eating, drinking, or smoking in public during daylight hours. Learning basic Arabic or French phrases is appreciated.

Visa & Entry

Citizens of many countries including the US, UK, EU, Canada, and Australia can enter Morocco visa-free for up to 90 days. Your passport must be valid for at least 6 months. No vaccinations are required for entry, though hepatitis A and typhoid are recommended. Morocco is generally very safe for tourists; standard precautions against petty theft in busy areas are sufficient.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the Morocco Discovery itinerary.

The best times to visit Morocco are spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) when temperatures are pleasant across the country. Summer (June-August) brings extreme heat to the Sahara and inland cities like Marrakech and Fez, with temperatures exceeding 40 degrees Celsius. Winter (December-February) is mild on the coast but cold in the Atlas Mountains with possible snow. For this itinerary covering desert and mountains, spring and autumn offer the most comfortable conditions.

Morocco is generally very safe for tourists and is one of the most visited countries in Africa. The main tourist areas of Marrakech, Fez, Chefchaouen, and the Sahara Desert are well-policed. Common sense precautions apply: be aware of petty theft in crowded souks, use licensed guides, and negotiate prices before purchasing. Moroccan hospitality is legendary and most visitors experience nothing but warmth and friendliness. Women traveling alone should dress modestly and be prepared for occasional unwanted attention in busy areas.

Morocco is a Muslim country, so modest dress is appreciated, especially outside tourist areas. For women, loose-fitting clothing covering shoulders and knees is recommended. In the medinas and souks, comfortable walking shoes are essential as streets are uneven. For the Sahara Desert, wear layers as nights are cold while days are hot, plus a scarf to protect from sand. In the Atlas Mountains, bring warm layers and sturdy hiking boots. Swimwear is fine at hotel pools and beaches but not in public areas.
