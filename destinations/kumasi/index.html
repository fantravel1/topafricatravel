Kumasi: The Garden City of West Africa

Ghana's cultural heartland and the seat of the Ashanti Kingdom, where centuries of royal tradition, master craftsmanship, and vibrant market culture thrive.

Manhyia Palace: Seat of the Ashanti King

Manhyia Palace is the official residence of the Asantehene, the king of the Ashanti people, and the spiritual center of one of Africa's most powerful historic kingdoms. The original palace, built in 1925 by the British as part of the return from exile of King Prempeh I, is now a museum showcasing the history of Ashanti royalty through photographs, personal effects, and ceremonial objects. The current Asantehene, Otumfuo Osei Tutu II, resides in the adjacent modern palace and holds court during the spectacular Akwasidae festival every six weeks.

Kejetia Market: West Africa's Trading Heart

Kejetia Market is one of the largest open-air markets in West Africa, a vast, labyrinthine marketplace where thousands of traders sell everything from fresh produce, spices, and textiles to traditional medicine, beads, and household goods. The market covers over 12 hectares and is organized by product, so entire sections are devoted to fabrics, another to foodstuffs, and others to handcrafted goods. Navigating Kejetia is an overwhelming sensory experience and the ultimate immersion in Ghanaian commercial culture. A local guide is invaluable for finding the best treasures.

Kente Weaving in Bonwire

The village of Bonwire, just outside Kumasi, is the legendary birthplace of kente cloth, one of the most iconic and recognizable textiles in the world. According to Ashanti legend, two hunters learned the art of weaving by watching a spider (Ananse) spinning its web. Today, master weavers sit at narrow wooden looms, their hands and feet working in mesmerizing rhythm to produce strips of silk and cotton fabric with intricate geometric patterns. Each kente design has a specific name and symbolic meaning, often referencing proverbs, historical events, or moral teachings.

Living Ashanti Culture

Kumasi is the beating heart of Ashanti culture, which remains one of the most vibrant and well-preserved traditional cultures in Africa. The Ashanti Kingdom, founded in the late 17th century, once rivaled European empires in wealth, military power, and administrative sophistication. Today, Ashanti traditions of chieftaincy, festivals, and craftsmanship continue to shape daily life. The Prempeh II Jubilee Museum houses royal regalia including a replica of the sacred Golden Stool, war trophies, and ceremonial objects that tell the story of this remarkable civilization.

Things to Do in Kumasi

From royal palaces to artisan villages, Kumasi offers an unparalleled journey into the heart of Ashanti heritage.

🏰

Manhyia Palace Museum

Tour the former palace of the Ashanti kings, viewing royal photographs, personal effects, and ceremonial objects, then witness the grounds where the Akwasidae festival unfolds.

🛒

Kejetia Market

Plunge into one of West Africa's largest open-air markets, navigating endless stalls of fabrics, spices, beads, and traditional goods in an electrifying sensory experience.

🎨

Bonwire Kente Weaving

Visit the birthplace of kente cloth and watch master weavers create dazzling silk and cotton textiles on traditional narrow-strip looms, each pattern rich with symbolic meaning.

🏛

Prempeh II Museum

Explore the museum housing a replica of the sacred Golden Stool, royal Ashanti regalia, war trophies, and artifacts documenting the kingdom's rise to power and resistance against colonialism.

🖌

Ntonso Adinkra Village

Watch artisans carve calabash stamps and hand-print adinkra symbols onto cloth using natural tree bark dye. Try your hand at stamping your own adinkra design to take home.

🏭

Ahwiaa Woodcarving Village

Observe skilled woodcarvers transforming raw timber into traditional Ashanti stools, fertility dolls (akuaba), drums, and decorative masks using hand tools passed down through generations.

🎸

Akwasidae Festival

Witness the spectacular royal ceremony held every six weeks where the Asantehene holds court in full regalia with drumming, dancing, and sub-chiefs paying homage in their finest kente.

🗼

Okomfo Anokye Sword

Visit the legendary sword driven into the ground by the Ashanti Kingdom's founding priest, Okomfo Anokye, which according to tradition cannot be pulled out and protects the nation.

Getting There

Kumasi Airport (KMS) receives domestic flights from Accra (45 minutes) operated by Africa World Airlines and PassionAir. The road from Accra to Kumasi takes approximately 4-5 hours via the well-maintained N6 highway. VIP and STC intercity buses offer comfortable, affordable service with multiple daily departures. Shared taxis (trotros) are the budget option. The journey passes through the Ashanti region's lush cocoa-growing landscape. Within Kumasi, shared taxis and trotros are the main transport, though ride-hailing apps are increasingly available.

Best Time to Visit

The dry season from November to March offers the most comfortable conditions for exploring Kumasi, with warm temperatures around 25-32°C and minimal rainfall. December and January may bring Harmattan haze from the Sahara but remain dry. The rainy season (April to October) sees frequent afternoon thunderstorms that can flood unpaved roads to craft villages, though mornings are typically dry. Time your visit to coincide with an Akwasidae festival for the most immersive cultural experience; check dates locally as they follow the traditional 42-day Akan calendar cycle.

Money & Costs

Kumasi is very affordable for international visitors. Budget travelers can manage on $25-40 per day with guesthouses and local restaurants. Mid-range hotels cost $40-100 per night. Meals at local chop bars (eateries) cost 15-30 GHS. A full-day craft village tour with a guide and transport runs 200-400 GHS. Manhyia Palace entry is 50 GHS. Cash is essential in markets and craft villages; ATMs are available in the city center and at major banks. Bargaining is expected and part of the cultural experience at Kejetia Market.

Local Tips

Hire a local guide for Kejetia Market; it is enormous and overwhelming without one. When visiting Manhyia Palace, dress respectfully and avoid wearing black or red, which are mourning colors in Ashanti culture. Ask permission before photographing people, especially at ceremonies. Learn a few Twi greetings: "Maakye" (good morning) and "Medaase" (thank you) go a long way. Try local dishes like fufu with light soup, kelewele (spiced plantain), and waakye (rice and beans). The best kente prices are found directly in Bonwire village rather than in Kumasi shops.

Frequently Asked Questions About Kumasi

Answers to the most common questions about visiting Kumasi.

The best time to visit Kumasi is during the dry season from November to March, when rainfall is minimal and temperatures are warm at 25-32°C. December and January can be hazy due to the Harmattan wind carrying Saharan dust, but the dry conditions are ideal for exploring outdoor markets and craft villages. The rainy season (April to October) brings frequent afternoon downpours that can make unpaved roads muddy, though mornings are usually dry. If you want to witness an Akwasidae festival at Manhyia Palace, these occur every six weeks on a Sunday and are held year-round regardless of weather.

Kente is a hand-woven silk and cotton fabric that originated with the Ashanti people and is one of Africa's most recognizable textiles. The cloth features vibrant geometric patterns in bold colors, with each design carrying specific symbolic meaning related to Ashanti history, philosophy, and proverbs. The village of Bonwire, approximately 18 kilometers from Kumasi, is the birthplace and primary production center of kente cloth. Here you can watch master weavers working on traditional narrow-strip looms, learn about the symbolism of different patterns, and purchase authentic kente directly from the craftspeople.

The Akwasidae is a sacred festival held every six weeks (following the Akan 42-day calendar cycle) at Manhyia Palace in Kumasi. During the celebration, the Asantehene (Ashanti King) holds court in full regalia, seated on a replica of the legendary Golden Stool, while sub-chiefs and queen mothers pay their respects in a display of drumming, dancing, and ceremonial pageantry. The festival honors ancestors, reaffirms political alliances, and celebrates Ashanti cultural identity. Visitors are welcome but should dress respectfully, ask permission before photographing, and follow the guidance of palace officials.

Two to three days is sufficient to experience Kumasi's main attractions. On day one, visit Manhyia Palace Museum in the morning, then explore the vast Kejetia Market and the Prempeh II Jubilee Museum. On day two, take a craft village tour: visit Bonwire for kente weaving, Ntonso for adinkra cloth stamping, and Ahwiaa for woodcarving. If you have a third day, visit the Kumasi Fort and Military Museum, the Okomfo Anokye Sword site, and the KNUST campus with its cultural center. Time your visit to coincide with an Akwasidae festival (every six weeks) for the most culturally immersive experience.
