Accra: West Africa's Rising Star

A fast-growing, culture-rich capital where deep-rooted traditions meet contemporary African creativity and boundless energy.

Historic Jamestown

Jamestown is the soul of Accra, a historic fishing district founded in the 17th century around the British-built James Fort. Climb the Jamestown Lighthouse for panoramic views of the coastline, watch fishermen haul in their daily catch on the beach, explore the colonial-era architecture, and experience the raw, unfiltered energy of one of West Africa's oldest neighborhoods. During August, the Chale Wote Street Art Festival transforms Jamestown into an extraordinary open-air gallery.

Independence Square & National Identity

Independence Square (Black Star Square) is the symbolic heart of Ghanaian nationhood. This vast public plaza, dominated by the Black Star Gate arch, commemorates Ghana's historic independence in 1957, the first sub-Saharan African nation to break free from colonial rule. Nearby, the Kwame Nkrumah Memorial Park honors Ghana's founding father with a striking mausoleum, museum, and tranquil gardens surrounded by fountains.

A City That Never Sleeps

Accra's nightlife is legendary across Africa. From rooftop bars in Osu and East Legon to open-air Afrobeats clubs in Airport Residential and live highlife music venues in Labadi, the city pulses with energy after dark. The annual Detty December season (late November through January) draws the global Ghanaian diaspora home for a non-stop calendar of concerts, beach parties, and cultural events that have made Accra December's hottest destination.

The Jollof Rice Capital

Ghanaians will tell you without hesitation that their jollof rice is the best in the world, and a visit to Accra is your chance to judge for yourself. This beloved tomato-based rice dish, cooked with spices, onions, and often served with fried chicken or grilled tilapia, is a point of fierce national pride. Beyond jollof, Accra's food scene spans from bustling street-food stalls serving waakye and kelewele to innovative fine-dining restaurants reinterpreting traditional Ghanaian cuisine.

Things to Do in Accra

From historic landmarks to vibrant markets, Accra delivers authentic West African experiences at every turn.

🏭

Jamestown

Explore Accra's oldest neighborhood. Climb the Jamestown Lighthouse for panoramic views, visit James Fort, watch fishermen on the beach, and soak in the district's raw, authentic atmosphere.

🏛

Independence Square

Stand at the Black Star Gate, Ghana's symbol of freedom as the first sub-Saharan nation to achieve independence. Explore the vast plaza used for national celebrations and parades.

🛒

Osu Oxford Street

Stroll Accra's liveliest commercial street. Browse boutiques selling Ghanaian fashion and textiles, sample street food, visit art galleries, and enjoy the vibrant cafe culture.

🏖

Labadi Beach

Relax at Accra's most popular beach. Enjoy live music, horse riding on the sand, fresh coconut water from vendors, and a festive weekend atmosphere with drumming and dancing.

🎲

Makola Market

Dive into the organized chaos of Accra's largest market. Thousands of vendors sell everything from colorful kente cloth and beads to spices, fresh produce, and traditional medicines.

🏙

Kwame Nkrumah Memorial

Visit the mausoleum and museum honoring Ghana's founding father and pan-African visionary. The park features fountains, gardens, and powerful exhibits on Ghana's path to independence.

🎨

National Museum of Ghana

Discover Ghana's rich cultural heritage through exhibits on Ashanti gold, traditional textiles, archaeological artifacts, and contemporary Ghanaian art spanning centuries of history.

🎶

Chale Wote Festival & Nightlife

Experience Accra's world-famous street art festival in Jamestown each August, then dance until dawn at Afrobeats clubs, rooftop lounges, and live highlife music venues across the city.

Getting There

Kotoka International Airport (ACC) is Accra's main gateway, receiving direct flights from major European, Middle Eastern, and African hubs. Airlines including British Airways, KLM, Emirates, Ethiopian Airlines, South African Airways, and Kenya Airways serve the airport. The airport is located just 10 km from the city center, and the ride takes 20-45 minutes depending on traffic. Uber and Bolt are reliable and affordable for airport transfers.

Best Time to Visit

The dry season (November-March) offers the most comfortable weather for exploring Accra, with warm days, lower humidity, and Harmattan haze from the Sahara adding a unique atmospheric quality. December is peak season thanks to the Detty December festivities. The rainy season (April-July, September-October) brings afternoon thunderstorms but also lush greenery and fewer tourists. August is ideal for the Chale Wote festival and Homowo traditional celebrations.

Money & Costs

Accra is reasonably priced for international visitors. Budget travelers can manage on $30-50 per day with guesthouses and local eateries. Mid-range hotels with tours run $80-150 per day. Upscale accommodations in Airport Residential and East Legon start at $200+. Mobile money (MTN MoMo) is widely used alongside cash. ATMs are readily available. Credit cards are accepted at hotels and upscale restaurants but not at markets or smaller establishments.

Local Tips

Use Uber or Bolt for reliable transport, as Accra traffic can be intense. Always greet people before beginning a conversation, as courtesy is deeply valued in Ghanaian culture. Dress respectfully when visiting traditional areas or chiefs' palaces. Carry cash for markets and street food. The right hand is used for greetings and eating. Tipping of 10% is appreciated at restaurants. Try to visit Makola Market on a weekday morning for the best experience and fewer crowds.

Frequently Asked Questions About Accra

Answers to the most common questions about visiting Accra.

The best time to visit Accra is during the dry season from November to March, when temperatures range from 25-32°C with lower humidity and minimal rainfall. December is particularly popular due to the festive Afrochella and Detty December events that draw the Ghanaian diaspora home. The main rainy season runs from April to July, with a shorter one in September-October. However, Accra can be visited year-round as rain typically comes in short afternoon bursts rather than all-day downpours.

Accra is one of the safest cities in West Africa for tourists. Ghanaians are famously welcoming, and the country's stable democracy contributes to a secure environment. Standard precautions apply: watch your belongings in crowded areas like Makola Market, avoid displaying expensive electronics, and use registered taxis or ride-hailing apps like Bolt at night. Areas popular with tourists such as Osu, East Legon, Airport Residential, and Labadi are well-patrolled. Solo travelers, including women, generally report feeling safe in Accra.

Chale Wote is Accra's largest street art festival, held annually in August in the historic Jamestown neighborhood. The festival transforms the streets into an open-air gallery featuring live art installations, body painting, graffiti murals, performance art, live music, film screenings, and spoken word. Founded in 2011, it has grown into one of Africa's most important contemporary art events, attracting thousands of visitors and showcasing Ghanaian and pan-African creativity. The festival is free to attend and runs over several days.

Accra is a food lover's paradise. The undisputed star is Ghanaian jollof rice, a richly spiced tomato rice dish that Ghanaians passionately claim is the best version in West Africa. Other must-try dishes include waakye (rice and beans with spicy shito sauce), banku and tilapia (fermented corn dough with grilled fish), kelewele (spiced fried plantains), and red-red (bean stew with fried plantain). For street food, head to Osu Night Market for grilled kebabs, or try the beloved chichinga (spiced meat skewers) from roadside vendors.
