Zanzibar: Where History Meets Paradise

A tropical archipelago steeped in Swahili culture, aromatic spices, and surrounded by some of the Indian Ocean's most beautiful beaches.

Stone Town: A Living Museum

Stone Town is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the cultural heart of Zanzibar. Its labyrinthine alleyways wind past ornately carved wooden doors, crumbling Arab mansions, bustling bazaars, and the former Sultan's Palace. This is where African, Arab, Indian, and European influences have converged for over a millennium. Visit the House of Wonders, the Old Fort, and the Anglican Cathedral built on the site of the last slave market in East Africa.

Pristine Beaches & Turquoise Waters

Zanzibar's coastline is ringed with some of the world's most stunning beaches. Nungwi Beach on the northern tip offers consistent swimming regardless of tides, crystal-clear turquoise waters, and spectacular sunsets. Kendwa is renowned for its full moon parties and calm waters. Paje on the east coast is a global kitesurfing hotspot, while the secluded beaches of Matemwe offer tranquil seclusion and world-class reef snorkeling just offshore.

The Spice Island Heritage

Zanzibar earned its nickname as the Spice Island through centuries of spice cultivation and trade. Guided spice tours take you through lush plantations where cloves, nutmeg, cinnamon, black pepper, vanilla, lemongrass, and cardamom grow in abundance. You'll learn to identify plants by scent and taste, discover traditional medicinal uses, and sample freshly prepared spice-infused dishes. These tours provide a fragrant journey through the island's agricultural soul.

Jozani Forest & Red Colobus Monkeys

Jozani Chwaka Bay National Park is Zanzibar's only national park and the last stronghold of the endangered Zanzibar red colobus monkey, found nowhere else on Earth. Wooden boardwalks wind through ancient mangrove swamps and tropical forest where these charismatic primates swing through the canopy overhead. The park also shelters Sykes monkeys, bush babies, over 50 butterfly species, and rare endemic birds in a compact but incredibly biodiverse ecosystem.

Things to Do in Zanzibar

From ancient ruins to coral reefs, Zanzibar offers unforgettable experiences for every type of traveler.

🏛

Stone Town Walking Tour

Get lost in the winding alleyways of this UNESCO heritage site, discovering intricately carved doors, the Old Fort, House of Wonders, and the historic slave market memorial.

🏖

Nungwi Beach

Relax on Zanzibar's premier beach with powdery white sand, turquoise waters unaffected by tides, and breathtaking sunsets framed by traditional dhow sailing boats.

🌿

Spice Tour

Visit lush plantations to see, smell, and taste cloves, vanilla, cinnamon, nutmeg, and cardamom. Learn centuries-old cultivation methods and enjoy a spice-infused Swahili lunch.

🐢

Prison Island

Take a short boat ride to Changuu Island to meet giant Aldabra tortoises, some over 100 years old. Snorkel in the surrounding crystal-clear waters and explore the ruins of the old quarantine station.

🌳

Jozani Forest

Trek through Zanzibar's only national park to spot the rare endemic red colobus monkeys swinging through the canopy, then walk the mangrove boardwalk through the ancient swamp forest.

⛵

Dhow Sunset Cruise

Sail aboard a traditional wooden dhow as the sun dips below the Indian Ocean horizon. Many cruises include fresh seafood, tropical fruit, and stops for swimming in secluded coves.

🎨

Freddie Mercury House

Visit the birthplace and childhood home of the legendary Queen frontman in Stone Town. The building now houses a small museum with memorabilia celebrating Zanzibar's most famous son.

🌊

Snorkeling & Diving

Explore vibrant coral reefs teeming with tropical fish at Mnemba Atoll, one of East Africa's premier dive sites, or snorkel the shallow reefs off Nungwi and Kendwa beaches.

Getting There

Abeid Amani Karume International Airport (ZNZ) receives direct flights from Dar es Salaam, Nairobi, Doha, and several European cities seasonally. High-speed ferries from Dar es Salaam take 1.5 to 2 hours, with multiple daily departures by Azam Marine. Most nationalities can obtain a visa on arrival or apply online via the Tanzania e-visa system. Many visitors combine Zanzibar with a mainland safari, flying in from the Serengeti or Kilimanjaro.

Best Time to Visit

The dry season from June to October brings warm, rain-free days perfect for beach holidays and diving, with temperatures around 25-28°C. December to February is hotter at 28-33°C with mostly clear skies. Avoid the long rains from March to May when heavy downpours can disrupt travel and some hotels close. The short rains in November are usually brief afternoon showers that rarely spoil a holiday. Diving visibility is best from October to March.

Money & Costs

Zanzibar is moderately priced for a tropical island destination. Budget travelers can manage on $50-80 per day with guesthouses in Stone Town and local restaurants. Mid-range beachfront hotels run $100-250 per night. Luxury resorts start at $300 and can exceed $1,000. US dollars are widely accepted alongside Tanzanian Shillings. ATMs are available in Stone Town but less common elsewhere. Spice tours cost around $25, and diving trips start at $60 per dive.

Local Tips

Zanzibar is predominantly Muslim, so dress modestly when walking through Stone Town and villages. Cover shoulders and knees away from the beach. Bargaining is expected in markets and with taxi drivers. Always agree on a price before getting into a taxi. The evening Forodhani Gardens night food market is a must-visit for affordable, delicious street food. Carry cash for smaller establishments. Malaria precautions are recommended, and tap water should be avoided.

Frequently Asked Questions About Zanzibar

Answers to the most common questions about visiting Zanzibar.

The best time to visit Zanzibar is during the two dry seasons: June to October and December to February. June to October offers warm, dry weather with temperatures around 25-28°C, ideal for beach holidays and diving. December to February is hotter at 28-33°C with clear skies. The long rains from March to May bring heavy downpours and some hotels close. The short rains in November are lighter and shouldn't deter visitors. For the best diving visibility, visit between October and March.

You can reach Zanzibar by air or by ferry from Dar es Salaam. Abeid Amani Karume International Airport receives direct flights from Dar es Salaam (20 minutes), Nairobi, and several international destinations. High-speed ferries from Dar es Salaam take approximately 1.5 to 2 hours, with several daily departures operated by Azam Marine and Kilimanjaro Fast Ferries. Book ferry tickets in advance during peak season. Some visitors also fly directly to Zanzibar from safari destinations like the Serengeti or Kilimanjaro.

Zanzibar is generally safe for tourists. Stone Town and the resort beaches are well-traveled areas with good security. Standard precautions apply: avoid walking alone late at night in dimly lit areas of Stone Town, keep valuables secure, and use reputable tour operators for excursions. Zanzibar is a predominantly Muslim island, so dressing modestly (covering shoulders and knees) when away from the beach shows respect for local culture. Swim only at beaches with calm waters, as some areas have strong currents and tidal changes.

Most visitors spend five to seven days in Zanzibar to enjoy a mix of culture and relaxation. Spend two days exploring Stone Town's historic sites, markets, and Freddie Mercury's house. Dedicate one day to a spice tour and one day to a trip to Prison Island or a dhow cruise. Reserve two to three days for beach relaxation, snorkeling, or diving at Nungwi, Kendwa, or Paje. If you want to visit Jozani Forest and The Rock Restaurant, add an extra day. Many travelers combine Zanzibar with a mainland Tanzania safari.
