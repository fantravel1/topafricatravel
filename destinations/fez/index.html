Fez: The Soul of Morocco

A living medieval city where centuries-old traditions of craftsmanship, scholarship, and Moroccan hospitality continue to thrive.

Fez el-Bali: The Ancient Medina

Fez el-Bali is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the world's largest car-free urban zone, with over 9,000 narrow alleys, lanes, and dead ends forming a seemingly impenetrable labyrinth. Founded in the 9th century, this living medieval city is home to nearly 200,000 residents, hundreds of mosques, workshops, and souks. Donkeys and handcarts remain the primary means of transport. The medina's sensory overload of sights, sounds, and smells creates one of the most immersive travel experiences on Earth.

Chouara Tannery: Ancient Craft

The Chouara Tannery is Fez's most iconic sight and one of the world's oldest, operating since the 11th century. From the terraces of surrounding leather shops, visitors gaze down at a honeycomb of stone vats filled with vivid natural dyes: saffron yellow, indigo blue, poppy red, and mint green. Workers wade barefoot through the pits, scraping and dyeing hides using the same techniques employed for nearly a thousand years. The pungent smell of pigeon droppings used in the curing process is unforgettable.

Al-Qarawiyyin: World's Oldest University

Founded in 859 AD by Fatima al-Fihri, Al-Qarawiyyin is recognized by UNESCO and the Guinness Book of Records as the world's oldest continuously operating university. This extraordinary institution has been a center of Islamic learning for over 1,100 years, producing scholars, philosophers, and scientists who shaped world history. The mosque complex features stunning Andalusian-Moroccan architecture, though non-Muslims can only admire it from the doorways. The recently restored library opened limited public access to its precious manuscripts.

Master Artisans & Pottery Workshops

Fez is Morocco's undisputed capital of traditional craftsmanship. The city's artisans produce exquisite zellige tilework, intricate brass lanterns, hand-woven textiles, and the famous blue-and-white Fassi pottery. Visit the Pottery Cooperative on the outskirts to watch master craftsmen shape, paint, and fire ceramics using techniques unchanged for centuries. In the medina, workshops specializing in leather, wood carving, and embroidery welcome visitors to observe their painstaking work and purchase directly from the makers.

Things to Do in Fez

From ancient medinas to artisan workshops, Fez offers a journey through Morocco's richest cultural heritage.

🏛

Fez el-Bali Medina

Plunge into the world's largest car-free urban zone, navigating 9,000 alleys past ancient mosques, bustling souks, hidden riads, and centuries-old fondouks with a licensed guide.

🎨

Chouara Tannery

Witness the ancient art of leather dyeing from surrounding rooftop terraces, where hundreds of stone vats hold vivid natural dyes in a scene unchanged since the 11th century.

🏫

Al-Qarawiyyin University

Admire the exterior of the world's oldest continuously operating university, founded in 859 AD. Peer through ornate doorways at the stunning Andalusian-Moroccan courtyard and fountain.

🕌

Bou Inania Madrasa

Marvel at this 14th-century masterpiece of Marinid architecture featuring intricate stucco carvings, cedar wood screens, zellige tilework, and a rare water clock mechanism.

🍣

Pottery Workshop

Visit the Fez Pottery Cooperative to watch artisans hand-paint the city's signature blue-and-white ceramics, learn about traditional kiln-firing techniques, and create your own piece.

🍴

Moroccan Cooking Class

Learn to prepare authentic tagine, couscous, and pastilla in a traditional riad kitchen. Shop for ingredients in the spice souk and enjoy the fruits of your labor on a rooftop terrace.

🌄

Merenid Tombs

Climb to the hilltop ruins of the Merenid dynasty tombs for the most spectacular panoramic views over the entire medina, especially beautiful at sunset or early morning.

🚲

Day Trip to Volubilis & Meknes

Explore the remarkably preserved Roman ruins of Volubilis, a UNESCO site, and the imperial city of Meknes with its monumental Bab Mansour gate, all within an easy day trip from Fez.

Getting There

Fez-Saiss Airport (FEZ) receives flights from major European cities including Paris, London, Barcelona, and Amsterdam, primarily via Ryanair, Royal Air Maroc, and Transavia. The airport is 15 km south of the city, with taxi rides to the medina costing around 150 MAD. Morocco's ONCF train network connects Fez to Casablanca (4 hours), Rabat (3 hours), Marrakech (7 hours), and Tangier (4 hours). CTM and Supratours buses also link Fez to all major cities.

Best Time to Visit

Spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) offer the most comfortable temperatures at 20-28°C, ideal for walking the medina's steep narrow streets. Summer temperatures can exceed 40°C, making extended walks exhausting. Winter (December-February) is cool at 8-16°C with occasional rain but very few tourists. The Fez Festival of World Sacred Music, usually held in June, is worth braving the heat for its world-class performances in stunning historic venues.

Money & Costs

Fez offers exceptional value for money. Budget travelers can manage on $30-50 per day with basic riads and local eateries. Beautiful mid-range riads with courtyards run $60-150 per night. Luxury riads and palaces start at $200. A medina guide costs 300-500 MAD for a half day. Meals at local restaurants range from 30-80 MAD. Cash is king in the medina; carry small denominations. ATMs are available at Bab Boujloud and in the Ville Nouvelle. Bargaining is expected in all souks.

Local Tips

Hire an official licensed guide (with brass badge) for your first medina visit. Wear comfortable flat shoes for the cobblestone streets. When you hear "balak!" step aside for passing donkeys or carts. Dress modestly, covering shoulders and knees. The best rooftop terraces for viewing the tanneries are in leather shops; you'll be expected to browse but not obligated to buy. Stay in a riad within the medina for the most authentic experience. Download offline maps as GPS can be unreliable in the narrow alleys.

Frequently Asked Questions About Fez

Answers to the most common questions about visiting Fez.

The best time to visit Fez is during spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) when temperatures are pleasantly warm at 20-28°C, perfect for exploring the medina on foot. Summer (June to August) can be extremely hot with temperatures exceeding 40°C, making walks through the narrow streets uncomfortable. Winter (December to February) is cooler at 8-16°C with occasional rain but far fewer tourists. The Fez Festival of World Sacred Music in June is a cultural highlight worth planning around despite the heat.

While not strictly necessary, hiring an official licensed guide is highly recommended for your first visit to Fez el-Bali. The medina contains over 9,000 alleys and lanes with no vehicle access, making it the world's largest car-free urban zone and extremely easy to get lost in. An official guide (identifiable by their brass badge) will navigate the labyrinth, explain the historical significance of key sites, and help you avoid unofficial guides and touts. Half-day guided tours typically cost 300-500 MAD. After an initial guided tour, many visitors feel confident exploring independently.

Fez is generally safe for tourists. The medina is heavily policed and well-monitored. The most common annoyance is unofficial guides and shop touts who may follow visitors; a polite but firm refusal usually suffices. Keep valuables close in crowded areas of the medina, and be cautious of pickpockets in busy souks. Walking at night in the medina is generally safe along main arteries but avoid poorly lit alleys. Women traveling alone may experience unwanted attention but serious incidents are rare. Use only official guides with brass identification badges.

Two to three full days is ideal for exploring Fez. On day one, dedicate a full day to Fez el-Bali with a licensed guide, visiting the Chouara tannery, Al-Qarawiyyin University exterior, Bou Inania Madrasa, and the souks. On day two, explore the Dar Batha Museum, the Jewish Quarter (Mellah), and the Royal Palace gates, then enjoy a traditional Moroccan cooking class. A third day allows for a pottery workshop in the Fez Pottery Cooperative, the Merenid Tombs for panoramic views, and a relaxing hammam experience. Day trips to Volubilis and Meknes are also possible.
